Nothing was the same.

In 1961, IBM killed the traditional typewriter model. Their new Selectric typewriter didn't have keybars. Or a moving carriage. Or ink ribbons out in the open. Instead, a spherical typing element wizzed across the page, imprinting letters at an unprecedented speed. It was called the typeball, and it came in over a dozen typefaces.

Since the 1800s, typewriters functioned by striking a typebar against paper when the corresponding key was pressed.

Additionally, only one monospaced typeface was at the user's disposal—no variation was available on the market.

As the typist worked through copy, the typewriter's carriage would move one space to the left with each additional keystroke.

The Typeball

For seven years, IBM's engineers labored to perfect the Selectric's signature component: the typeball, also referred to as the “golf ball” and “single typing element.” IBM president Thomas Watson, Jr. labeled it as “the most totally distinct invention we've ever made as a company.” Each character was assigned binary code for tilt and rotation; engineers had also experimented with a mushroom form.

Instead of the carriage moving to accommodate additional text, the typewriter's housing remained completely still as the typeball worked its way across the page—all at blazing speed.

Typists could resume from exactly where they left off. Consumer typefaces included a variety of serifs, sans serifs, and scripts.

The Selectric's housing could be easily opened to allow for a quick and simple typeface change.

The Composer

Before the personal computer, professional typesetting required an investment of hundreds of thousands of dollars in specialized phototypesetting equipment. To streamline the process and appeal to smaller businesses, IBM released the Selectric Composer family of devices. Using unique, variable-width typefaces and magnetic tape, body copy could be produced with professional results.

First, copy was typed on the basic, monospaced Magnetic Typewriter which recorded each character on magnetic tape.

Finally, justification settings were calculated and set, and the Magnetic Composer read the tape, automatically outputting the copy onto paper. The operator would need to be present to oversee end-of-line decisions such as hypenation.

The finalized tape was then removed and placed into the computer's second bay; corrections could be implemented by cutting and replacing the corresponding section of tape.
